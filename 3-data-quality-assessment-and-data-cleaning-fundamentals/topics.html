3 - Data Quality Assessment and Data Cleaning Fundamentals — Module Topics

Introduction to Data Quality

Explores the concept of data quality and why it matters in data analysis. Covers the key dimensions of data quality including accuracy, completeness, consistency, and reliability.
  • What Is Data Quality? — Data quality refers to the degree to which a dataset is fit for its intended purpose in analysis, decision-making, or reporting.
  • Why Data Quality Matters in Analysis — The reliability of any data analysis is directly dependent on the quality of the underlying data used to produce it.
  • Accuracy as a Dimension of Data Quality — Accuracy refers to how closely the values in a dataset reflect the true, real-world values they are intended to represent.
  • Completeness as a Dimension of Data Quality — Completeness measures the extent to which all required data values are present and available within a dataset.
  • Consistency as a Dimension of Data Quality — Consistency ensures that data values are uniform and do not contradict each other across records, systems, or time periods.
  • Reliability as a Dimension of Data Quality — Reliability refers to the trustworthiness and stability of a data source — that is, whether the data consistently produces the same values under the same conditions.
  • The Interrelationship of Data Quality Dimensions — The dimensions of data quality — accuracy, completeness, consistency, and reliability — are interconnected and must be evaluated together for a comprehensive quality assessment.

Identifying Missing Values

Examines how to detect and understand missing data within a dataset. Discusses the types of missingness and their potential impact on analytical outcomes.
  • What Are Missing Values? — Missing values occur when no data is stored for a variable in a particular observation, often represented as NULL, NaN, blank cells, or placeholder codes.
  • Types of Missingness: MCAR, MAR, and MNAR — Statistical theory classifies missing data into three mechanisms — Missing Completely At Random (MCAR), Missing At Random (MAR), and Missing Not At Random (MNAR) — each with different implications for analysis.
  • Detecting and Quantifying Missing Data — Before deciding how to handle missing values, analysts must measure how much data is missing and identify which variables and rows are most affected.
  • Impact of Missing Values on Analytical Outcomes — Unaddressed missing data can distort statistical summaries, reduce statistical power, and introduce bias into predictive models or conclusions.
  • Documenting and Communicating Missing Data Findings — A critical but often overlooked step is systematically documenting where, how much, and why data is missing, so that downstream users and stakeholders understand the dataset's limitations.

Handling Duplicate Data

Covers strategies for identifying and removing duplicate records that can skew analysis results. Explains how duplicates arise and the best practices for resolving them.
  • What Are Duplicate Records? — Duplicate records occur when the same data entry appears more than once in a dataset, either as exact copies or as near-identical rows with minor variations.
  • Common Causes of Duplicate Data — Understanding why duplicates arise helps analysts prevent them at the source and apply the right resolution strategy.
  • Identifying Duplicate Records — Detection is the critical first phase of duplicate handling, requiring both automated tools and domain knowledge to surface true duplicates.
  • Strategies for Resolving Duplicates — Once duplicates are identified, analysts must decide how to resolve them based on context, data completeness, and business rules.
  • Impact of Duplicates on Analysis — Unresolved duplicates can distort virtually every type of analysis, from simple counts to machine learning model training.
  • Best Practices for Duplicate Prevention and Documentation — A proactive approach to duplicates combines preventive data governance with thorough documentation of all cleaning decisions made.

Detecting and Managing Outliers

Introduces methods for identifying outliers and anomalies in datasets using statistical and visual techniques. Discusses when to remove, transform, or retain outliers based on context.
  • What Are Outliers and Why They Matter — Outliers are data points that deviate significantly from the rest of a dataset and can distort analysis results if not properly addressed.
  • Statistical Methods for Detecting Outliers — Statistical techniques provide objective, quantifiable criteria for flagging data points that fall outside expected ranges.
  • Visual Techniques for Identifying Outliers — Visual exploration of data provides an intuitive and effective complement to statistical outlier detection methods.
  • Deciding Whether to Remove Outliers — Removing outliers should be a deliberate, context-driven decision rather than an automatic step in the cleaning process.
  • Transforming Outliers as an Alternative Approach — When outliers are genuine but extreme, transformation techniques can reduce their influence without discarding potentially valuable data.
  • Retaining Outliers When Context Demands It — In many analytical contexts, outliers carry critical information and must be retained to produce valid and complete results.
  • Best Practices for Outlier Management — A systematic, well-documented approach to outlier management ensures data cleaning decisions are reproducible, defensible, and aligned with analytical goals.

Resolving Data Inconsistencies

Focuses on finding and correcting inconsistent data entries such as formatting errors, conflicting values, and non-standard representations. Provides techniques for standardizing data across a dataset.
  • Understanding Data Inconsistencies — Data inconsistencies occur when the same information is represented in different, conflicting, or non-standard ways across a dataset.
  • Identifying Formatting Errors — Formatting errors involve data that contains the correct information but is recorded in a non-uniform structure or style.
  • Detecting Conflicting Values — Conflicting values occur when two or more fields within the same record contain information that logically contradicts each other.
  • Standardizing Categorical and Text Data — Standardization ensures that all entries representing the same concept use a single, consistent format or label throughout the dataset.
  • Normalizing Dates, Numbers, and Units — Numerical and date fields often suffer from inconsistencies in units of measurement, precision, or format that must be resolved before analysis.
  • Techniques for Automated Inconsistency Correction — Automated techniques can efficiently resolve large-scale inconsistencies by applying rule-based transformations or mapping dictionaries programmatically.
  • Documenting and Validating Inconsistency Resolutions — Every inconsistency correction should be documented and validated to maintain data integrity and support reproducibility of the cleaning process.

Systematic Data Cleaning Strategies

Presents a structured, step-by-step approach to cleaning raw datasets from initial assessment through validation. Emphasizes repeatable workflows and documentation best practices.
  • Initial Dataset Assessment — Before any cleaning begins, a thorough assessment of the raw dataset establishes a baseline understanding of its structure, size, and quality issues.
  • Identifying and Handling Missing Values — Missing values are one of the most common data quality problems and must be located and addressed systematically before analysis.
  • Detecting and Removing Duplicate Records — Duplicate rows can distort analysis results by overrepresenting certain observations and must be identified and resolved early in the cleaning workflow.
  • Correcting Inconsistencies and Standardizing Formats — Inconsistent formatting and encoding of values—such as mixed date formats or varied category labels—must be standardized to ensure reliable downstream analysis.
  • Detecting and Treating Outliers — Outliers are values that deviate significantly from the rest of the data and can indicate either genuine rare events or data entry errors requiring correction.
  • Establishing a Repeatable Cleaning Workflow — A systematic, scripted cleaning workflow ensures that the same cleaning steps can be applied consistently when new data arrives or when the process must be audited.
  • Validating Cleaned Data and Documentation Best Practices — After cleaning is complete, validation checks confirm that the dataset meets expected quality standards before it is used for analysis or modeling.

Preparing Clean Data for Analysis

Covers the final steps in data preparation, ensuring cleaned datasets are reliable, well-structured, and ready for downstream analysis. Discusses validation checks and quality assurance techniques.
  • Final Validation Checks — Before a dataset is considered ready for analysis, it must pass a series of final validation checks to confirm that all cleaning steps were applied correctly and completely.
  • Structural Integrity and Consistency — A clean dataset must be structurally sound, meaning columns are consistently typed, named, and organized in a way that supports reliable downstream processing.
  • Outlier Review and Final Treatment — As part of final preparation, any remaining outliers should be reviewed to determine whether they represent valid extreme values or residual data errors requiring treatment.
  • Quality Assurance Techniques — Quality assurance (QA) techniques provide a systematic framework for verifying that the cleaned dataset meets predefined quality standards before it is released for analysis.
  • Documentation and Data Lineage — Comprehensive documentation of all cleaning steps and decisions creates a transparent record that enables others to understand, reproduce, and trust the cleaned dataset.
  • Splitting and Formatting for Downstream Use — The final step in preparation involves formatting and structuring the cleaned dataset to meet the specific requirements of the intended analysis tool, model, or reporting system.
  • Establishing a Repeatable Cleaning Pipeline — Building a repeatable and automated cleaning pipeline ensures that the same quality standards are applied consistently whenever new data is introduced.
