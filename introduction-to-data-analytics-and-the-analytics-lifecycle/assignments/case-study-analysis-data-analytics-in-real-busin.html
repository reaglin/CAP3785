Case Study Analysis: Data Analytics in Real Business Practice

Module: Introduction to Data

Assignment Type: Case Study Analysis (Individual)

Estimated Effort: 2–4 hours

Submission Format: As directed by your instructor or learning management system

TILT Framework

The following three sections explain why you are doing this assignment, what you need to do, and how your work will be evaluated. Read all three sections before you begin.

Purpose — Why This Assignment Matters

Data analytics is no longer confined to technology companies or research institutions. Organizations of every size and industry — retail, healthcare, logistics, finance, and beyond — now rely on data-driven decisions to remain competitive and serve customers effectively. Understanding the type of analytics being applied, and the specific business question it answers, is a foundational skill for anyone working with data or managing teams that do.

This assignment connects directly to the module's central learning outcome: the ability to distinguish among descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, and prescriptive analytics by explaining the specific business question each type addresses. By researching a real business case, you will move beyond definitions and see how these analytics types function in practice — anchoring abstract concepts to observable outcomes and reinforcing your ability to apply this knowledge in your own career context.

Task — What You Will Do

You will research a real, named business that used data analytics to meet a specific organizational objective and write a concise, structured case study analysis. Full task instructions are provided in the Step-by-Step Instructions section below.

Criteria — How You Will Be Graded

Your paper will be assessed using the rubric at the end of this document. The rubric evaluates the accuracy and depth of your identification of the analytics type, the completeness of each required section, the quality of your explanation linking analytics type to business outcome, and the correctness of your citation.

Learning Outcome Addressed

Upon successful completion of this assignment, you will be able to:

  1. Distinguish among descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, and prescriptive analytics by correctly identifying which type was employed in your chosen business case and explaining the specific business question that type of analytics was designed to answer. (MO1 — Understand)

Key Terms — Defined

The following terms appear throughout this assignment. Review them carefully so you can apply them accurately in your paper.

  • Descriptive Analytics: Examines historical data to answer the question "What happened?" — for example, a sales dashboard showing last quarter's revenue by region.
  • Diagnostic Analytics: Digs deeper into historical data to answer "Why did it happen?" — for example, identifying that a sales drop was caused by a supply-chain disruption in a specific product category.
  • Predictive Analytics: Uses statistical models or machine learning to answer "What is likely to happen?" — for example, forecasting customer churn probability over the next 90 days.
  • Prescriptive Analytics: Recommends actions to answer "What should we do about it?" — for example, an optimization model that recommends the ideal inventory level for each warehouse location to minimize cost and stockouts.
  • Business Objective: The specific, measurable goal the organization was trying to achieve (e.g., reduce customer churn by 15%, cut logistics costs by $2 million, improve patient readmission rates).
  • Analysis Design: The overall approach or methodology used — including which type of analytics was applied and, at a high level, how the analysis was structured or conducted.
  • Source of Data: Where the data came from — for example, internal transaction records, customer surveys, sensor data, social media feeds, or third-party data providers.

Choosing Your Business Case

Important: You must select a real, named company and a real, documented use of data analytics. You may not invent or fabricate details. Every factual claim about the business and its analytics project must be supported by a verifiable reference.

Acceptable Sources

  • Company press releases, white papers, or case study pages on official company websites
  • Peer-reviewed journal articles or conference proceedings
  • Reputable news outlets (e.g., The Wall Street Journal, Harvard Business Review, MIT Sloan Management Review, Forbes)
  • Industry analyst reports (e.g., Gartner, McKinsey, Deloitte Insights)
  • Technology vendor case studies that name the client company and describe measurable outcomes

Examples of Suitable Business Cases (for inspiration only — choose your own)

  • A major retailer using sales history dashboards to monitor store performance (descriptive)
  • A streaming platform analyzing why subscriber cancellations spiked after a content change (diagnostic)
  • An airline using machine learning to forecast seat demand and set dynamic ticket prices (predictive)
  • A logistics firm using optimization algorithms to recommend optimal delivery routes in real time (prescriptive)

Step-by-Step Instructions

Step 1 — Research Your Business Case (approximately 45–75 minutes)

Select a real business and locate at least one credible source documenting how that business applied data analytics to a specific objective. As you read, take notes on all seven elements listed in Step 2. Confirm you can answer each question from your sources before you begin writing.

Step 2 — Identify the Type of Analytics (approximately 15–20 minutes)

Before drafting your paper, explicitly identify which of the four analytics types — descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, or prescriptive — was primarily used in your case. Use the definitions in the Key Terms section as your guide. Ask yourself: What specific business question was this analytics project designed to answer? The answer to that question will confirm the analytics type. If more than one type was used, identify the primary type and briefly note any secondary types.

Step 3 — Write Your Case Study Analysis (approximately 60–90 minutes)

Write a concise, structured paper organized into the following seven numbered sections. Each section must be labeled with its heading exactly as shown below.

  1. Business — State the full, legal name of the company, its industry, and a one- to two-sentence description of what the company does. Do not assume your reader is familiar with the organization.
  2. Objective — State the specific business objective the analytics project was designed to achieve. Be precise: avoid vague phrases like "improve performance." Instead, write something like "reduce inventory holding costs by identifying slow-moving SKUs across 300 distribution centers."
  3. Analysis Design — Identify the type of analytics used (descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, or prescriptive) and explain, in two to four sentences, how that analytics type was applied to address the objective. Explicitly state the business question the analytics was designed to answer (e.g., "What is the likelihood that this customer will cancel their subscription in the next 30 days?").
  4. Source of Data — Describe where the data came from. Specify whether the data was internal (generated by the company's own operations), external (purchased or obtained from outside sources), or a combination. Note the general type of data (e.g., transaction records, sensor readings, social media feeds, demographic data).
  5. Results of Analysis — Summarize the findings or outputs of the analytics project. What did the analysis reveal, forecast, or recommend? Use quantitative results where available (e.g., "the model identified 12,000 at-risk customers with 87% accuracy").
  6. Effect on Business Practice — Explain how the results changed what the business actually did. Describe any measurable business outcomes (cost savings, revenue growth, operational improvements, customer satisfaction gains, etc.) that resulted from acting on the analytics. This section is where you connect the analytics project to real-world business impact.
  7. References — List all sources you used, including the source(s) that document the analytics case. Follow the citation format specified by your instructor. At minimum, provide enough information for a reader to locate each source independently: author(s) or organization name, publication title, website URL (if applicable), and date accessed or published.

Step 4 — Review and Submit (approximately 15–20 minutes)

Before submitting, review your paper using the checklist below. Check each item to confirm it is complete and accurate.

  • All seven sections are present and labeled correctly.
  • The analytics type is correctly identified and explicitly connected to a specific business question.
