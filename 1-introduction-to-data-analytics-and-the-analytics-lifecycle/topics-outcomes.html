Module Topics

What is Data Analytics?

Defines data analytics and establishes its purpose and importance in modern decision-making. Explores how organizations use data to gain insights and competitive advantage.
  • Defining Data Analytics — Data analytics is the process of examining, cleaning, transforming, and modeling data to discover useful information, draw conclusions, and support decision-making.
  • The Purpose of Data Analytics — The primary purpose of data analytics is to support better, faster, and more confident decision-making across all levels of an organization.
  • Why Data Analytics Matters Today — The explosive growth of digital data, combined with advances in computing power, has made data analytics one of the most valuable capabilities an organization can possess.
  • Gaining Insights from Data — Insights are the meaningful patterns, trends, and relationships uncovered through analysis that an organization can act upon to improve outcomes.
  • Competitive Advantage Through Analytics — Organizations that leverage data analytics effectively can outperform competitors by anticipating market shifts, optimizing operations, and delivering superior customer experiences.
  • Data Analytics Across Industries — Data analytics is applied across virtually every industry — from retail and finance to healthcare, manufacturing, and government — each using it to solve domain-specific problems.

The Four Types of Analytics

Introduces and distinguishes descriptive, diagnostic, predictive, and prescriptive analytics. Explains the unique role and value each type plays in the analytics spectrum.
  • Descriptive Analytics: Understanding What Happened — Descriptive analytics is the foundational type of analytics, focused on summarizing historical data to answer the question 'What happened?'
  • Diagnostic Analytics: Understanding Why It Happened — Diagnostic analytics goes a step beyond descriptive analytics by examining data to answer the question 'Why did it happen?'
  • Predictive Analytics: Forecasting What Will Happen — Predictive analytics uses statistical models and machine learning techniques to forecast future outcomes by answering 'What is likely to happen?'
  • Prescriptive Analytics: Recommending What Should Be Done — Prescriptive analytics is the most advanced type, combining predictions with optimization techniques to answer 'What should we do about it?'
  • The Analytics Spectrum: A Progressive Value Chain — The four types of analytics form a progressive spectrum, with each type building on the previous one and delivering increasing business value and complexity.

The CRISP-DM Process Framework

Walks through the Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining as a structured lifecycle for analytics projects. Covers each phase of the framework and how they interconnect.
  • Overview and Purpose of CRISP-DM — CRISP-DM, or the Cross-Industry Standard Process for Data Mining, is a widely adopted framework that provides a structured, repeatable lifecycle for planning and executing analytics projects.
  • Phase 1: Business Understanding — The first phase focuses on clearly defining the project objectives and requirements from a business perspective before any data work begins.
  • Phase 2: Data Understanding — Data understanding involves collecting initial data, exploring its characteristics, and identifying quality issues or interesting patterns early in the project.
  • Phase 3: Data Preparation — Data preparation encompasses all activities needed to construct the final dataset that will be used for modeling, including cleaning, transforming, and integrating data from various sources.
  • Phase 4: Modeling — In the modeling phase, analysts select and apply appropriate analytical or machine learning techniques to the prepared dataset and calibrate model parameters to optimize performance.
  • Phase 5: Evaluation — The evaluation phase assesses whether the model meets the original business objectives, not just technical performance metrics, before any decision is made to deploy it.
  • Phase 6: Deployment and the Iterative Nature of CRISP-DM — Deployment is the final phase, in which the validated model or analytic findings are integrated into business processes so that stakeholders can act on the results.

Real-World Business Applications

Examines how data analytics is applied across industries to solve practical business problems. Illustrates the tangible value analytics delivers in organizational contexts.
  • Retail and E-Commerce Optimization — Retailers use data analytics to understand customer purchasing behavior, manage inventory, and personalize shopping experiences at scale.
  • Healthcare and Patient Outcomes — The healthcare industry applies analytics to improve patient care, reduce costs, and predict disease risk before symptoms become critical.
  • Financial Services and Risk Management — Banks and financial institutions rely on analytics to assess credit risk, detect fraud, and guide investment decisions in real time.
  • Supply Chain and Operations Management — Organizations use analytics to improve supply chain visibility, reduce operational costs, and respond faster to disruptions across logistics networks.
  • Marketing and Customer Analytics — Marketing teams leverage data analytics to measure campaign effectiveness, understand customer journeys, and allocate budgets toward the highest-impact channels.
  • Manufacturing and Quality Control — Manufacturers apply analytics to monitor equipment performance, reduce defects, and move from reactive maintenance to proactive operational strategies.
  • The Tangible Business Value of Analytics — Across all industries, the common thread is that analytics converts raw data into actionable insight that drives measurable improvements in efficiency, revenue, and decision-making.

Foundational Case Studies

Presents concrete examples of analytics in action through foundational case studies. Connects theoretical concepts to real outcomes achieved through data-driven approaches.
  • Retail Demand Forecasting with Predictive Analytics — Retailers have used predictive analytics to anticipate customer demand, optimize inventory levels, and reduce waste. This case study illustrates how historical sales data can be transformed into actionable forecasts.
  • Healthcare Diagnostics and Patient Outcome Improvement — Healthcare organizations have applied diagnostic and predictive analytics to identify at-risk patients and improve clinical outcomes. These case studies show how data-driven decisions can have life-saving consequences.
  • Financial Services Fraud Detection — Banks and credit card companies use real-time analytics to detect fraudulent transactions and protect customers. This case study connects prescriptive analytics to immediate, automated decision-making.
  • Sports Performance Analytics — Professional sports teams have embraced analytics to evaluate player performance, inform draft decisions, and develop game strategies. This case study made analytics concepts accessible to mainstream audiences.
  • E-Commerce Personalization and Recommendation Engines — Online retailers like Amazon have built recommendation systems that use customer behavior data to suggest relevant products, directly increasing revenue. This case study highlights the business impact of prescriptive analytics at scale.
  • Connecting Case Studies to the Analytics Lifecycle — Each foundational case study reflects the structured process described by frameworks like CRISP-DM, from defining a business problem to deploying and evaluating a solution. Recognizing this connection reinforces why process discipline matters in analytics work.
