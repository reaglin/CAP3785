Module Topics

Foundations of Data Visualization

Introduces the purpose and importance of data visualization in communicating information effectively. Covers the core principles that guide good visual design decisions.
  • The Purpose of Data Visualization — Data visualization transforms raw numbers and information into visual formats that make patterns, trends, and insights easier to understand and communicate.
  • Choosing the Right Chart Type — Selecting an appropriate chart type is one of the most critical decisions in data visualization, as the wrong choice can obscure meaning or mislead viewers.
  • Visual Hierarchy and Emphasis — Visual hierarchy guides the viewer's eye to the most important elements first, ensuring the key message is communicated before supporting details.
  • Core Principles of Good Visual Design — Effective data visualizations follow established design principles that prioritize accuracy, simplicity, and the honest representation of data.
  • Avoiding Misleading Representations — Poorly designed or intentionally manipulated visualizations can distort perception and lead audiences to incorrect conclusions, even when the underlying data is accurate.
  • The Relationship Between Data and Audience — Effective visualization requires understanding who will view the chart and what decisions or insights they need to take away from it.

Choosing the Right Chart Type

Explores the major chart types and the specific data relationships each is best suited to represent. Provides a framework for selecting the most appropriate visualization for a given dataset.
  • Understanding the Purpose of Chart Selection — Choosing the right chart type begins with identifying the specific data relationship or message you want to communicate.
  • Bar and Column Charts for Comparisons — Bar and column charts are best suited for comparing discrete categories or ranking values against one another.
  • Line Charts for Trends Over Time — Line charts are the standard choice when showing how a continuous variable changes across an ordered sequence, most commonly time.
  • Pie and Donut Charts for Part-to-Whole Composition — Pie and donut charts show how individual parts contribute to a total, making them appropriate only when the segments sum to a meaningful whole.
  • Scatter Plots for Relationships Between Variables — Scatter plots reveal correlations, clusters, or outliers by plotting two continuous numeric variables against each other on the x and y axes.
  • A Decision Framework for Chart Selection — A simple four-category framework — comparison, composition, distribution, relationship — helps analysts quickly narrow down the appropriate chart type for any dataset.

Visual Hierarchy and Design Principles

Examines how layout, color, size, and emphasis guide a viewer's attention through a chart. Covers best practices for creating clear, readable, and well-organized visuals.
  • Understanding Visual Hierarchy — Visual hierarchy is the principle of arranging chart elements so that the viewer's eye naturally moves from the most important information to supporting details.
  • Using Color Purposefully — Color is one of the most powerful tools for directing attention and encoding meaning in a chart, but it must be applied with restraint and intention.
  • Size and Scale as Emphasis Tools — Varying the size of chart elements — such as fonts, markers, or chart areas — signals relative importance and guides the viewer's focus.
  • Layout and Spatial Organization — The spatial arrangement of chart elements — titles, legends, axes, and data — determines how intuitively a viewer can read and interpret the visual.
  • Emphasis Through Contrast and Weight — Contrast — differences in color, tone, thickness, or typography — is the primary mechanism for making specific data points or messages stand out.
  • Clarity and Readability Best Practices — A chart is only effective if its audience can read and understand it quickly; clarity should always take priority over decorative complexity.
  • Applying Design Principles in Excel — Excel provides built-in formatting tools that allow designers to implement visual hierarchy and design best practices directly within the charting environment.

Avoiding Misleading Representations

Identifies common ways charts can distort or misrepresent data, such as truncated axes or inappropriate scaling. Teaches students to critically evaluate and ethically construct accurate visual displays.
  • Truncated Axes and False Impressions — One of the most common ways charts mislead viewers is by starting the y-axis at a value other than zero, which exaggerates differences between data points.
  • Inappropriate Scaling and Dual Axes — Manipulating the scale of an axis or using dual axes with different scales can make unrelated trends appear correlated or disguise the true relationship between variables.
  • Cherry-Picking Data Ranges — Selecting only a convenient subset of the available data — such as a specific time window — can misrepresent long-term trends and mislead the audience.
  • Distorted Proportions in Pie and Area Charts — Pie charts and area-based visualizations can distort perceived proportions when slices are exploded, 3D effects are applied, or areas are used instead of lengths to encode values.
  • Misleading Chart Type Selection — Choosing a chart type that does not match the nature of the data can inherently misrepresent relationships, trends, or comparisons.
  • Ethical Responsibilities in Data Visualization — Constructing charts carries an ethical obligation to represent data honestly, especially when visualizations are used to inform decisions or influence opinion.
  • Critically Evaluating Charts You Encounter — Developing the skill to detect misleading visualizations in real-world media and reports is just as important as knowing how to build accurate ones.

Creating Charts in Excel

Provides step-by-step guidance on building and formatting charts using Excel's charting tools. Covers inserting chart types, adjusting data ranges, and applying formatting options.
  • Inserting a Chart in Excel — Excel provides a straightforward process for inserting charts directly from your data using the Insert tab.
  • Choosing the Right Chart Type — Excel offers a wide variety of chart types, and selecting the correct one is essential for accurately conveying your data's story.
  • Adjusting the Chart Data Range — After inserting a chart, you may need to modify the data range it references to correct errors or expand the visualization.
  • Applying Chart Layouts and Styles — Excel's Chart Design tab provides pre-built layouts and styles that quickly change the overall appearance of a chart.
  • Formatting Individual Chart Elements — Beyond preset styles, Excel allows you to format specific chart elements individually for precise control over the visualization's appearance.
  • Adding and Editing Chart Titles and Labels — Descriptive titles and axis labels are critical for making a chart self-explanatory and communicating context to the audience.
  • Moving and Resizing Charts — Charts in Excel can be repositioned on the same worksheet or moved to a dedicated chart sheet for cleaner presentation.

Building Effective Visual Summaries in Excel

Demonstrates how to combine charts and supporting elements into cohesive visual summaries within Excel. Focuses on applying visualization principles to produce polished, professional outputs.
  • Selecting the Right Chart Type for Your Summary — Choosing an appropriate chart type is the foundation of an effective visual summary in Excel.
  • Applying Visual Hierarchy to Chart Elements — Visual hierarchy guides the viewer's eye to the most important information first within your Excel chart or dashboard.
  • Combining Multiple Chart Elements into a Cohesive Layout — A polished visual summary often integrates charts, titles, annotations, and supporting text into a unified Excel layout.
  • Using Color Purposefully and Consistently — Color is one of the most powerful yet frequently misused tools in data visualization, and Excel provides full control over chart color schemes.
  • Adding Titles, Labels, and Annotations for Clarity — Descriptive titles, axis labels, and data annotations transform a raw chart into a self-explanatory visual summary.
  • Avoiding Misleading Visual Representations — Professional visual summaries require careful attention to chart formatting choices that can unintentionally distort or misrepresent data.
  • Reviewing and Refining for a Polished, Professional Output — The final step in building an effective visual summary is a deliberate review process to ensure clarity, consistency, and professional quality.
